This database represents a representative sample of published DAC sorbents — not a complete catalog — and will be continuously updated as new studies are published.

Recorded Parameters

Each entry in the database corresponds to one experimental measurement of a sorbent under a specific set of conditions. A total of 18 parameters were recorded for each entry, drawn directly from the corresponding publication. Text and numerical values were extracted from the paper text; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported in the paper are marked nr; values not applicable to a given measurement type are marked na.

Performance Metrics

CO₂ Adsorption Capacity

The CO₂ uptake of the sorbent, converted to mmol CO₂ per gram of sorbent. Capacities were first recorded in the units reported by each paper (mmol/g, wt%, g CO₂/g sorbent, etc.) and then converted. For isotherm data, the capacity at or near 400 ppm CO₂ was used. Capacities that could not be converted to mmol/g (for example, mmol/g-amine without a reported amine weight fraction) are marked nr.

Degradation Rate (%/cycle)

The average percent reduction in CO₂ capture capacity per adsorption–desorption cycle. Defined as:

Degradation rate (%/cycle)  =  q1 − qlast⁄q1  ×  100⁄N
where q1 = CO₂ capacity of the first cycle, qlast = capacity of the last cycle, N = total number of cycles tested.

This metric does not account for nonlinear degradation as a function of cycle number or differences in cycle duration. However, given the scarcity of degradation data, it allows a large enough subset of sorbents to be analyzed and is achievable to report for most researchers. Only sorbents tested for ≥2 cycles where both first and last cycle capacities are reported are included.

Cycle Time (calculated) CALCULATED

A normalized kinetic metric to compare adsorption dynamics across different breakthrough reactor setups. Not directly recorded from papers — derived from other reported parameters. Since mass loading and gas flow rate depend on column dimensions, direct comparison of saturation times across studies is not straightforward. Instead, CO₂ uptake efficiency is assumed to be independent of flow rate, mass loading, and CO₂ concentration, allowing the saturation time to be modeled for a standard reactor:

CO₂ uptake efficiency  =  q × m⁄t95 × F × C / 22.4
q = CO₂ capacity (mmol/g),  m = mass loading (g),  t95 = time to 95% outlet (min),  F = flow rate (mL/min),  C = CO₂ concentration (ppm)

Standard conditions for normalization: mass = 100 mg, flow rate = 400 mL/min, CO₂ = 400 ppm. The standardized cycle time is then:

Cycle time  =  t95,std × 3

Cycle time can only be calculated for breakthrough experiments that report t95, mass loading, flow rate, CO₂ concentration, and CO₂ capacity.

Sorbent Identity

Year

Year of publication of the paper in which the sorbent data was reported.

Sorbent Class

Classification of the sorbent based on the active CO₂-capture functionality. Amine: any material with an amine functional group that interacts with CO₂, regardless of the support material type. MOF: crystalline metal–organic frameworks that do not contain amine groups participating in CO₂ binding. Metal oxide: inorganic compounds of metals and oxygen. Other: all remaining sorbent types, including COFs, zeolites, ionic liquids, and moisture-swing sorbents.

Material Abbreviation

A short identifier for the sorbent, using the abbreviation from the source paper where available. Additional descriptors (e.g., loading percentage, functional group type) are included where necessary to distinguish similar materials from the same study.

Experimental Conditions

Capture Temperature

The temperature (°C) at which CO₂ adsorption was performed. For sorbents reported only as "room temperature" or "ambient conditions," a value of 25 °C was recorded.

Release Temperature

The temperature (°C) used during the regeneration or desorption phase. For materials that undergo activation or degassing rather than a thermal desorption cycle, the activation temperature was recorded. For vacuum-swing materials, this is the temperature at which vacuum was applied during regeneration.

CO₂ Concentration

The CO₂ concentration (ppm) in the feed gas during the adsorption phase. For sorbents reported only as "ambient air," a value of 425 ppm was used. Only sorbents tested below 5,000 ppm CO₂ were included in the database.

Capture Gas

Whether oxygen was present in the capture gas during adsorption, recorded as Y or N. N (Inert + CO₂): the feed gas is an inert carrier (e.g., N₂ or He) with CO₂ added, with no oxygen present. Y (Air): the feed gas contains O₂, representing simulated or real ambient air. This distinction is important because O₂ can drive oxidative degradation of amine sorbents, and sorbents tested only under inert gas may report overestimated stabilities.

Regeneration Gas

The gas used during the desorption or regeneration phase. Common values include N₂, He, Ar (inert), vacuum, and gas mixtures containing CO₂ or H₂O vapor. Recorded as reported in the paper.

Relative Humidity

The relative humidity (%) of the capture feed gas during adsorption. Also converted to mole percent water vapor and dew point (°C) using the recorded adsorption temperature. A value of 0% indicates fully dry conditions.

Number of Cycles

The total number of adsorption–desorption cycles performed during the experiment. A value of 0 indicates that only a single adsorption measurement was made with no cycling. Approximately 20% of sorbents in the database report any cycling data at all.

Degradation Cause

The mechanism of degradation as reported or suggested in the paper. Categories include: Thermal, Oxidation, Hydrolysis, CO₂-catalyzed, Pore blockage, Structural, Negligible degradation, No mechanism suggested, and combinations of multiple causes. If multiple degradation causes were reported, the sorbent is included in all relevant categories.

CO₂ Measurement Method

The experimental technique used to measure CO₂ uptake. Gravimetric (TGA): the mass change of the sorbent is tracked over time in a thermogravimetric analyzer as CO₂-containing gas flows over a small (~mg-scale) sample. Breakthrough: the CO₂ outlet concentration is monitored as gas flows through a packed-bed column; sorption dynamics are governed by both intrinsic kinetics and column hydrodynamics. Volumetric (Isotherm): equilibrium CO₂ uptake is measured volumetrically at defined partial pressures. Other: all remaining methods.

Gas Flow Rate

The volumetric flow rate (mL/min) of the capture gas during adsorption. Not applicable for isotherm measurements. Large variation in flow rates across studies — especially between amine-based sorbents (typically 100–500 mL/min) and MOFs (often below 50 mL/min) — complicates direct comparison of kinetic data.

Time to 95% Outlet Concentration (t₉₅)

The time (min) for the CO₂ outlet concentration of a breakthrough column to reach 95% of the inlet CO₂ concentration. This is the primary kinetic metric recorded from breakthrough experiments and is used to calculate the standardized cycle time. Only applicable to breakthrough experiments.

Column Diameter

The inner diameter (cm) of the breakthrough column. Only applicable to breakthrough experiments. Required, along with mass loading and flow rate, to normalize kinetic data across different reactor geometries.

Mass of Sorbent

The mass (g) of sorbent loaded into the column or TGA sample pan. Wide variation in this parameter — from less than 0.1 g to more than 10 g across studies — significantly affects reported adsorption times and must be accounted for when comparing kinetic data.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability and deployment-relevance of DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

A key challenge in assessing progress in DAC sorbent development is the wide variation in testing conditions used across studies. Without a consistent testing framework, differences in reported CO₂ capacity, kinetics, and degradation may reflect experimental differences rather than true material improvements.

When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, every sorbent class appears to show a positive trend over time. But once only sorbents tested under the same standard conditions are isolated, only amine sorbents show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries under standard conditions to analyze. This underscores why standard conditions are essential.

To determine recommended standard conditions, we identified the most frequently reported (mode) value for each experimental parameter within each sorbent class. The percentage shown in parentheses indicates the fraction of sorbents of that class that were tested under that condition.

How Many Sorbents Meet Standard Conditions?

Applying the standard conditions as sequential filters reveals how quickly the dataset narrows. For all 914 sorbents combined, filtering by regeneration gas, CO₂ concentration, capture gas, humidity, capture temperature, and regeneration temperature leaves only 335 sorbents (37%) satisfying all standard conditions simultaneously.

The situation varies by class. For amine sorbents, 312 of 710 (44%) meet all standard conditions. For MOF sorbents, only 4 of 42 (10%) do. For metal oxide sorbents, 15 of 56 (27%) qualify, but zero have sufficient breakthrough data to calculate cycle time. This highlights that while amine sorbents have a reasonable comparable dataset, MOFs and metal oxides are severely under-characterized at standard conditions.

Reporting Kinetics and Degradation

CO₂ capacity is available for ~96% of sorbents, but cycle time can only be calculated for ~12% and degradation rate for only ~4%. These metrics do not require additional experiments — cycle time can be derived from existing breakthrough data if flow rate, mass loading, and t₉₅ are also reported, and degradation rate only requires extending the number of cycles and reporting first and last cycle capacities.

Recommendation: We encourage researchers to adopt standard conditions analogous to NREL's photovoltaic testing program, where defined reference conditions enabled reproducible comparison of solar cell efficiencies across institutions worldwide. Always report all experimental conditions alongside performance data — even small differences in capture temperature, humidity, or CO₂ concentration can substantially alter measured values.

Deployment-Relevant Reporting

While standardized testing enables comparability across studies, real DAC systems will operate under a wide range of environmental conditions. A sensitivity analysis on Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying humidity, ambient temperature, and altitude across realistic U.S. deployment sites can shift the modeled net CO₂ removal cost by more than $400 per ton.

Currently, only about 8% of sorbents in the database have been characterized under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative gas environments, humid gas environments, and breakthrough measurements. This is a significant gap in the field.

Key Environmental Factors

Relative Humidity

Water has a strong and complex influence on amine sorbent performance. At moderate humidity (20–60% RH), water can increase CO₂ capacity by shifting the reaction mechanism from carbamate formation toward ammonium bicarbonate, improving amine efficiency. At high humidity, excess water loading raises regeneration energy requirements and can block CO₂ access to binding sites.

Ambient Temperature

Lower ambient temperatures increase CO₂ adsorption affinity and equilibrium capacity but also raise heating requirements for desorption. At higher temperatures, competitive water adsorption reduces working CO₂ capacity. DAC deployment sites span from hot-dry desert environments to cold subarctic regions, and sorbents should be evaluated across a representative temperature range.

Altitude and CO₂ Partial Pressure

Although the atmospheric mixing ratio of CO₂ remains nearly constant throughout the troposphere, total pressure decreases with altitude, reducing CO₂ partial pressure. At sea level, ~420 ppm CO₂ corresponds to ~0.42 mbar; at 2,000 m elevation, the same concentration corresponds to ~0.34 mbar. We recommend reporting CO₂ isotherms across 0.35–0.45 mbar to capture conditions relevant to both high-altitude and sea-level deployment sites.

Oxygen Exposure

DAC systems operate in ambient air (~21% O₂). Oxygen, particularly at elevated regeneration temperatures, can cause oxidative degradation of amine functional groups. Sorbents tested only under inert conditions may significantly overestimate real-world stability. Degradation testing should be performed under simulated air conditions during both adsorption and regeneration.

What to Report

  • CO₂ capacity and kinetics at multiple humidity levels (at minimum: 0% and one moderate level such as 40–60% RH)
  • CO₂ capacity at multiple capture temperatures
  • CO₂ capacity across a CO₂ partial pressure range of 0.35–0.45 mbar
  • Degradation testing under simulated air during both adsorption and regeneration
  • At least 10 adsorption–desorption cycles with first and last cycle capacities explicitly reported

Varying working CO₂ capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy demand, and sorbent lifetime by just ±30% can shift the modeled levelized cost of CO₂ removal by several hundred dollars per ton. Deployment-relevant testing is therefore not optional for sorbents intended for real-world application.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials alongside new sorbents is critical for ensuring measurement reliability and enabling long-term comparability. Over 60% of sorbents in our database were reported in only a single publication, making it difficult to verify measured CO₂ capacities or reproduce results. Regularly testing common benchmark materials would establish a network of reference data anchoring new measurements to a known standard.

Selection Approach

Benchmark candidates were identified from the database based on the following criteria: the material must be commercially available at a reasonable cost, must have been tested under the standard conditions for its sorbent class, and should have the highest deployment-relevance characterization among qualifying candidates. This ensures the benchmarks are both accessible and well-characterized for deployment modeling.

About This Database

Methods, team, and citation information.

Overview

This database was compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid direct air capture (DAC) sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included. This is a representative sample, not a complete catalog, and the database will be continuously updated.
