Recorded Parameters

Each entry in the database corresponds to one experimental measurement of a sorbent under a specific set of conditions. A total of 18 parameters were recorded for each entry, drawn directly from the corresponding publication. Text and numerical values were extracted from the paper text; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported in the paper are marked nr; values not applicable to a given measurement type are marked na.

Performance Metrics

CO₂ Adsorption Capacity

The CO₂ uptake of the sorbent, converted to mmol CO₂ per gram of sorbent. Capacities were first recorded in the units reported by each paper (mmol/g, wt%, g CO₂/g sorbent, etc.) and then converted. For isotherm data, the capacity at or near 400 ppm CO₂ was used. Capacities that could not be converted to mmol/g (for example, mmol/g-amine without a reported amine weight fraction) are marked nr.

Degradation Rate (%/cycle)

The average percent reduction in CO₂ capture capacity per adsorption–desorption cycle. Defined as:

Degradation rate (%/cycle) = q1 − qlast⁄q1 × 100⁄N
where q1 = CO₂ capacity of the first cycle, qlast = capacity of the last cycle, N = total cycles tested.

This metric does not account for nonlinear degradation as a function of cycle number or differences in cycle duration. However, given the scarcity of degradation data, it allows a large enough subset of sorbents to be analyzed and is achievable to report for most researchers. Only sorbents tested for ≥2 cycles where both first and last cycle capacities are given are included.

Cycle Time (calculated) CALCULATED

A normalized kinetic metric to compare adsorption dynamics across different breakthrough reactor setups. Not directly recorded from papers — derived from other reported parameters.

Since mass loading and gas flow rate depend on the column size, direct comparison of saturation times across studies is not possible. Instead, CO₂ uptake efficiency is assumed to be independent of flow rate, mass loading, and CO₂ concentration, allowing the saturation time to be modeled for a standard reactor configuration:

CO₂ uptake efficiency = q × m⁄t95 × F × C / 22.4
q = CO₂ capacity (mmol/g), m = mass loading (g), t95 = time to 95% outlet (min), F = flow rate (mL/min), C = CO₂ concentration (ppm)

Standard conditions used for normalization: mass = 100 mg, flow rate = 400 mL/min, CO₂ concentration = 400 ppm. The standardized cycle time is then:

Cycle time = t95,std × 3

Cycle time can only be calculated for breakthrough experiments that report t95, mass loading, flow rate, CO₂ concentration, and CO₂ capacity.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability and deployment-relevance of DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

A key challenge in assessing progress in DAC sorbent development is the wide variation in testing conditions used across studies. Without a consistent testing framework, differences in reported CO₂ capacity, kinetics, and degradation may reflect experimental differences rather than true material improvements.

When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, every sorbent class appears to show a positive trend over time. But once only sorbents tested under the same standard conditions are isolated, only amine sorbents show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries under standard conditions to analyze. This underscores why standard conditions are essential for drawing meaningful conclusions from the database.

To determine recommended standard conditions, we identified the most frequently reported (mode) value for each experimental parameter within each sorbent class. The results are shown below.

How Many Sorbents Meet Standard Conditions?

Applying the standard conditions as sequential filters reveals how quickly the dataset narrows. For all sorbents combined (914 total), progressively filtering by regeneration gas, CO₂ concentration, capture gas, humidity, capture temperature, and regeneration temperature leaves only 335 sorbents — about 37% of the database — that satisfy all standard conditions simultaneously.

The situation is more acute when broken down by class. For amine sorbents, 312 of 710 (44%) meet all standard conditions. For MOF sorbents, only 4 of 42 (10%) do. For metal oxide sorbents, the number drops to 15 of 56 (27%), and critically, zero metal oxide sorbents have been tested under standard conditions with sufficient breakthrough data to calculate cycle time. This highlights a major gap: while amine sorbents have a reasonable comparable dataset, MOFs and metal oxides are severely under-characterized at standard conditions, making cross-class comparisons unreliable.

Reporting Kinetics and Degradation

Beyond capacity, two additional metrics are essential for assessing sorbent performance: cycle time (a measure of adsorption kinetics) and degradation rate. Yet these are dramatically under-reported. CO₂ capacity is available for ~96% of sorbents, but cycle time can only be calculated for ~12% and degradation rate for only ~4%.

Importantly, these metrics do not require additional experiments. Cycle time can be derived from the same breakthrough experiment used to measure capacity, provided the gas flow rate, mass loading, and t₉₅ are also reported. Degradation rate simply requires extending the number of cycles and reporting the capacity at the first and last cycle. We strongly encourage researchers to report these parameters as standard practice.

Recommendation: Establish a standardized approach similar to NREL's photovoltaic testing program, where defined reference conditions (AM 1.5G, 25 °C, 1000 W/m²) enabled reproducible comparison of solar cell efficiencies across institutions worldwide. Applying the same philosophy to DAC sorbents would reduce measurement uncertainty, build confidence in reported performance, and allow genuine material improvements to be distinguished from measurement variability.

Deployment-Relevant Reporting

While standardized testing enables comparability across studies, real DAC systems will operate under a wide range of environmental conditions. A sensitivity analysis on the commercial amine sorbent Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying humidity, ambient temperature, and altitude across realistic U.S. deployment sites can shift the modeled net CO₂ removal cost by more than $400 per ton. This makes deployment-relevant testing not just scientifically valuable but economically critical.

Currently, only about 8% of sorbents in the database have been characterized under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative gas environments, humid gas environments, and breakthrough measurements. This is a major gap in the field.

Environmental Factors That Matter for Deployment

Relative Humidity

Water has a strong and complex influence on amine sorbent performance. At moderate humidity (20–60% RH), water can increase CO₂ capacity by shifting the reaction mechanism from carbamate formation toward ammonium bicarbonate, improving amine efficiency. However, at high humidity, excess water loading increases regeneration energy requirements and can block CO₂ access to binding sites. These effects mean that the optimal operating humidity is material-dependent and can only be determined through systematic humidity-resolved testing.

Ambient Temperature

Lower ambient temperatures increase CO₂ adsorption affinity and equilibrium capacity, but also raise the heating requirements for desorption, increasing energy demand. At higher temperatures, competitive water adsorption reduces working CO₂ capacity. DAC deployment sites span a wide range of climates — from hot-dry desert environments in the American Southwest to cold subarctic regions — and sorbents should be evaluated across a representative temperature range to capture these effects.

Altitude and CO₂ Partial Pressure

Although the atmospheric mixing ratio of CO₂ remains nearly constant throughout the troposphere, the total atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude, reducing the partial pressure of CO₂. At sea level, ~420 ppm CO₂ corresponds to a partial pressure of approximately 0.42 mbar; at 2,000 m elevation, the same concentration corresponds to only about 0.34 mbar. This reduction weakens the adsorption driving force and lowers equilibrium loading. We recommend reporting CO₂ isotherms across the range of 0.35–0.45 mbar to capture conditions relevant to both high-altitude and sea-level deployment sites.

Oxygen Exposure

DAC systems operate in ambient air, which contains ~21% O₂. Yet the majority of sorbents in the database were tested only under inert gas atmospheres during both adsorption and regeneration. Oxygen, particularly at elevated regeneration temperatures, can cause oxidative degradation of amine functional groups, leading to irreversible capacity loss. Sorbents tested only under inert conditions may significantly overestimate real-world stability. We recommend that degradation testing always be performed under simulated air conditions — both during adsorption and regeneration — to provide a realistic assessment of long-term sorbent performance.

What to Report

To enable deployment-relevant assessment, we recommend that researchers report sorbent performance under the following conditions in addition to the standard baseline:

  • CO₂ capacity and kinetics at multiple humidity levels (at minimum: 0%, and one moderate humidity condition such as 40–60% RH)
  • CO₂ capacity at multiple capture temperatures (at minimum: the standard condition and one higher temperature reflecting warm-climate deployment)
  • CO₂ capacity across a CO₂ partial pressure range of 0.35–0.45 mbar
  • Degradation testing under simulated air (with O₂ present) during both adsorption and regeneration
  • At least 10 adsorption–desorption cycles for any stability assessment, with first and last cycle capacities explicitly reported

Reliable technoeconomic modeling requires accurate estimates of working CO₂ capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy demand, and sorbent lifetime — all of which are sensitive to environmental conditions. Varying these parameters by just ±30% can shift the modeled levelized cost of CO₂ removal by several hundred dollars per ton. Deployment-relevant testing is therefore not optional for sorbents intended for real-world application.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials alongside new sorbents is critical for ensuring measurement reliability and enabling long-term comparability across laboratories. Over 60% of sorbents in our database were reported in only a single publication, making it difficult to verify measured CO₂ capacities or reproduce published results. Regularly testing a common set of benchmark materials would establish a network of reference data that anchors new measurements to a known standard.

Selection Approach

Benchmark candidates were identified from the database based on the following criteria: the material must be commercially available at reasonable cost, must have been tested under the standard conditions for its sorbent class, and must have the highest Deployment-Relevance characterization among qualifying candidates. This approach ensures the benchmarks are both accessible to researchers and well-characterized for deployment modeling. Based on these criteria, the following materials are recommended:

Lewatit VP OC 1065
Amine
A polyamine-functionalized polymer bead from Lanxess. The most widely studied amine sorbent in the database and the reference material used for the technoeconomic sensitivity analysis in this work. Commercially available and extensively characterized.
ZIF-8
MOF
A zeolitic imidazolate framework (Zn-based). Commercially available, thermally stable, and among the most studied MOFs for gas adsorption. Recommended as the MOF benchmark given its availability and breadth of existing characterization.
γ-Al₂O₃
Metal oxide
Gamma-alumina is a widely available metal oxide support with well-documented surface chemistry and CO₂ adsorption properties. Serves as an accessible metal oxide reference point for DAC conditions.
Zeolite 13X
Other
A commercially available sodium aluminosilicate zeolite widely used in gas separation. Well-characterized for CO₂ adsorption and recommended as the benchmark for the "other" sorbent class.

About This Database

Methods, team, and citation information.

Overview

This database was compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid direct air capture (DAC) sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening for relevance and experimental CO₂ capacity data below 5,000 ppm, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included. This is a representative sample, not a complete catalog of all DAC sorbents. The database will be continuously updated as new studies are published and as additional materials are added.
