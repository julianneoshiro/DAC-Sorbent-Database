Data collection

Recorded Parameters

18 parameters were recorded for each entry. Text and numerical values were extracted from each paper; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported: nr; not applicable: na. For performance metric equations, see the Trends page.

Year

Year of publication of the source paper.

Sorbent Class

Amine (any amine group interacting with CO₂), MOF (without amine CO₂-binding), Metal oxide, or Other (COFs, zeolites, ionic liquids, etc.).

Material Abbreviation

Short identifier from the source paper, with additional descriptors where needed.

CO₂ Adsorption Capacity

CO₂ uptake in mmol/g, converted from reported units. For isotherms, value at ~400 ppm CO₂.

Capture Temperature

Temperature (°C) during CO₂ adsorption. "Room temperature" recorded as 25 °C.

Release Temperature

Temperature (°C) during regeneration.

CO₂ Concentration

CO₂ concentration (ppm) in feed gas. "Ambient air" recorded as 425 ppm. Only below 5,000 ppm included.

Capture Gas

Y = Air (O₂ present); N = Inert + CO₂. O₂ can drive oxidative amine degradation.

Regeneration Gas

Gas used during desorption: N₂, He, Ar, vacuum, or mixtures with CO₂ or H₂O.

Relative Humidity

RH (%) of capture feed gas. Also converted to mole percent and dew point. 0% = dry.

Number of Cycles

Total adsorption–desorption cycles. Zero = single measurement. ~20% of sorbents report any cycling data.

Degradation Rate (%/cycle)

Average % capacity loss per cycle: [(q₁ − q_last)/q₁] × 100/N. Requires ≥2 cycles and first/last capacities.

Degradation Cause

Mechanism as reported: Thermal, Oxidation, Hydrolysis, CO₂-catalyzed, Pore blockage, Structural, Negligible, None suggested.

CO₂ Measurement Method

Gravimetric (TGA), Breakthrough (packed bed), Volumetric (isotherm), or Other.

Gas Flow Rate

Volumetric flow rate (mL/min) during adsorption. Not applicable for isotherm measurements.

t₉₅

Time (min) for breakthrough column outlet to reach 95% of inlet CO₂. Used to calculate cycle time.

Column Diameter

Inner diameter (cm) of the breakthrough column.

Sorbent Mass / Cycle Time

Mass (g) in column or TGA pan. Cycle time is calculated from standardized breakthrough parameters, not directly recorded.

Performance Trends Over Time

CO₂ Capture Capacity

How much CO₂ can a sorbent hold? CO₂ capture capacity — in mmol per gram — is the most fundamental performance metric and the most consistently reported in the literature.

An upward trend over time indicates genuine material progress, but only when comparing sorbents under the same testing conditions. When all conditions are included, the apparent trend reflects variation in experimental protocols as much as true improvements.

Filters

Cycle Time CALCULATED

How fast does a sorbent reach capacity? Cycle time normalizes adsorption kinetics across different reactor setups. Calculated from breakthrough experiments using a standardized reactor (mass=100 mg, flow=400 mL/min, CO₂=400 ppm):

Cycle time = t95,std × 3
CO₂ uptake efficiency assumed independent of reactor parameters.

A lower cycle time means faster kinetics — more cycles per day and higher DAC productivity.

Filters

Degradation Rate

Does the sorbent hold up? Only ~20% of sorbents have any cycling data. Degradation rate = average % capacity loss per cycle:

Deg. rate = q1−qlast⁄q1 × 100⁄N
q1=first cycle, qlast=last cycle, N=total cycles.

An apparent increase in recent years likely reflects a growing emphasis on stability testing rather than materials becoming less stable.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability and deployment-relevance of DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

A key challenge in assessing progress is the wide variation in testing conditions across studies. Without a consistent framework, differences in reported performance may reflect experimental variation rather than genuine material improvements.

When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, every class shows a positive trend. Once only sorbents under standard conditions are isolated, only amine sorbents show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries to analyze.

Standard conditions were identified as the most frequently reported (mode) value for each parameter within each class. The percentage in parentheses is the fraction tested under that condition.

How Many Sorbents Meet Standard Conditions?

Applying the standard conditions sequentially reduces 914 sorbents to just 335 (37%). Amine: 312/710 (44%). MOF: only 4/42 (10%). Metal oxide: 15/56 (27%), but zero have sufficient breakthrough data to calculate cycle time.

Recommendation: Adopt standard conditions analogous to NREL's photovoltaic testing program. Always report all experimental conditions — even small differences substantially alter measured performance values.

Deployment-Relevant Reporting

While standardized testing enables comparability, real DAC systems operate across wide environmental variation. A sensitivity analysis on Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying humidity, temperature, and altitude across U.S. deployment sites can shift the modeled net CO₂ removal cost by more than $400 per ton.

Only about 8% of sorbents have been characterized under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative environments, humid conditions, and breakthrough measurements.

Key Environmental Factors

Relative Humidity

At moderate humidity (20–60% RH), water can increase CO₂ capacity of amine sorbents by shifting the mechanism toward ammonium bicarbonate. At high humidity, excess water loading raises regeneration energy and blocks binding sites.

Ambient Temperature

Lower temperatures increase adsorption affinity but raise desorption energy. DAC sites range from hot-dry desert to cold subarctic conditions.

Altitude and CO₂ Partial Pressure

At 2,000 m elevation, CO₂ partial pressure is ~0.34 mbar vs. ~0.42 mbar at sea level. We recommend reporting CO₂ isotherms across 0.35–0.45 mbar.

Oxygen Exposure

DAC operates in ~21% O₂. Oxygen at elevated regeneration temperatures causes oxidative amine degradation. Stability testing must include simulated air.

What to Report

  • Capacity and kinetics at multiple humidity levels (min: 0% and ~40–60% RH)
  • Capacity at multiple capture temperatures
  • Capacity across CO₂ partial pressure 0.35–0.45 mbar
  • Degradation under simulated air during adsorption and regeneration
  • At least 10 cycles with first and last cycle capacities reported

Varying working capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy, and sorbent lifetime by ±30% can shift the modeled cost of CO₂ removal by hundreds of dollars per ton.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials is critical for measurement reliability and long-term comparability. Over 60% of sorbents were reported in only a single publication.

Selection Approach

Benchmarks were identified as commercially available materials tested under standard conditions for their class with the highest deployment-relevance characterization among qualifying candidates.

Lewatit VP OC 1065
Amine
Polyamine-functionalized polymer bead. Most widely studied amine sorbent and the reference for our technoeconomic sensitivity analysis.
ZIF-8
MOF
Zeolitic imidazolate framework (Zn-based). Commercially available, thermally stable, widely studied.
γ-Al₂O₃
Metal oxide
Widely available metal oxide support with well-documented CO₂ adsorption properties under DAC conditions.
Zeolite 13X
Other
Commercially available sodium aluminosilicate zeolite, well-characterized for CO₂ adsorption.

About the project

The Database

Compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid DAC sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included. This is a representative sample, not a complete catalog, and will be continuously updated.
