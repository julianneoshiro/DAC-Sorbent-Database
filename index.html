Recorded Parameters

Each entry corresponds to one experimental measurement under a specific set of conditions. A total of 18 parameters were recorded for each entry. Text and numerical values were extracted from each paper; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported are marked nr; not-applicable values are marked na.

For detailed descriptions of CO₂ Adsorption Capacity, Degradation Rate, and Cycle Time — including definitions, equations, and limitations — see the Trends page where each metric is described under its tab.

Sorbent Identity

Year

Year of publication of the paper in which the sorbent data was reported.

Sorbent Class

Classification based on the active CO₂-capture functionality. Amine: any material with an amine functional group that interacts with CO₂, regardless of support. MOF: crystalline metal–organic frameworks without amine CO₂-binding groups. Metal oxide: inorganic metal–oxygen compounds. Other: COFs, zeolites, ionic liquids, moisture-swing sorbents, and all remaining types.

Material Abbreviation

Short identifier from the source paper. Additional descriptors are included where necessary to distinguish similar materials from the same study.

Experimental Conditions

Capture Temperature

Temperature (°C) during CO₂ adsorption. Sorbents reported as "room temperature" or "ambient" were recorded as 25 °C.

Release Temperature

Temperature (°C) during regeneration. For vacuum-swing materials, the temperature at which vacuum was applied. For materials with activation rather than desorption, the activation temperature.

CO₂ Concentration

CO₂ concentration (ppm) in the feed gas. Sorbents reported as "ambient air" were recorded as 425 ppm. Only sorbents tested below 5,000 ppm were included.

Capture Gas

Whether oxygen was present during adsorption. N (Inert + CO₂): inert carrier with CO₂, no O₂. Y (Air): feed gas contains O₂. Oxygen can drive oxidative amine degradation, so inert-only testing may overestimate stability.

Regeneration Gas

Gas used during desorption. Common values: N₂, He, Ar, vacuum, or mixtures with CO₂ or H₂O. Recorded as reported.

Relative Humidity

Relative humidity (%) of the capture gas. Also converted to mole percent and dew point (°C). A value of 0% indicates dry conditions.

Number of Cycles

Total adsorption–desorption cycles performed. Zero = single adsorption, no cycling. ~20% of sorbents in the database report any cycling data.

Degradation Cause

Mechanism of degradation as reported. Categories: Thermal, Oxidation, Hydrolysis, CO₂-catalyzed, Pore blockage, Structural, Negligible, No mechanism suggested, and combinations. All applicable categories are listed.

CO₂ Measurement Method

Gravimetric (TGA): mass change in thermogravimetric analyzer. Breakthrough: CO₂ outlet concentration in packed-bed column. Volumetric (Isotherm): equilibrium uptake at defined partial pressures. Other.

Gas Flow Rate

Volumetric flow rate (mL/min) during adsorption. Not applicable for isotherm measurements.

t₉₅

Time (min) for breakthrough column CO₂ outlet to reach 95% of inlet concentration. Primary kinetic metric from breakthrough experiments, used to calculate cycle time.

Column Diameter

Inner diameter (cm) of the breakthrough column. Required alongside mass and flow rate to normalize kinetic data across reactor geometries.

Mass of Sorbent

Mass (g) loaded into the column or TGA pan. Ranges from <0.1 g to >10 g across studies, significantly affecting reported adsorption times.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability and deployment-relevance of DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

A key challenge in assessing progress in DAC sorbent development is the wide variation in testing conditions used across studies. Without a consistent framework, differences in reported performance may reflect experimental variation rather than genuine material improvements.

When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, every class appears to show a positive trend over time. But once only sorbents tested under the same standard conditions are isolated, only amine sorbents show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries to analyze.

Standard conditions were determined by identifying the most frequently reported (mode) value for each parameter within each sorbent class. The percentage in parentheses indicates the fraction of that class tested under that condition.

How Many Sorbents Meet Standard Conditions?

Applying the standard conditions sequentially reduces the full dataset of 914 sorbents to just 335 (37%). For amine sorbents, 312 of 710 (44%) qualify. For MOFs, only 4 of 42 (10%). For metal oxides, 15 of 56 (27%), but zero have sufficient breakthrough data to calculate cycle time.

Reporting Kinetics and Degradation

CO₂ capacity is available for ~96% of sorbents, but cycle time can only be calculated for ~12% and degradation rate for ~4%. These metrics do not require additional experiments — they can be derived from existing data if key parameters are also reported. We encourage researchers to report flow rate, mass loading, and t₉₅ alongside breakthrough capacity, and to extend cycling to at least 10 cycles.

Recommendation: Adopt standard conditions analogous to NREL's photovoltaic testing program. Always report all experimental conditions — even small differences can substantially alter measured performance values.

Deployment-Relevant Reporting

While standardized testing enables comparability, real DAC systems operate across a wide range of environmental conditions. A sensitivity analysis on Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying humidity, temperature, and altitude across U.S. deployment sites can shift the modeled net CO₂ removal cost by more than $400 per ton.

Only about 8% of sorbents in the database have been characterized under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative gas environments, humid conditions, and breakthrough measurements.

Key Environmental Factors

Relative Humidity

At moderate humidity (20–60% RH), water can increase CO₂ capacity of amine sorbents by shifting the reaction mechanism toward ammonium bicarbonate. At high humidity, excess water loading raises regeneration energy and blocks binding sites.

Ambient Temperature

Lower temperatures increase CO₂ adsorption affinity but raise desorption energy. Higher temperatures reduce capacity due to competitive water adsorption. DAC sites range from hot-dry desert to cold subarctic conditions.

Altitude and CO₂ Partial Pressure

At 2,000 m elevation, the CO₂ partial pressure is ~0.34 mbar vs. ~0.42 mbar at sea level, reducing adsorption driving force. We recommend reporting CO₂ isotherms across 0.35–0.45 mbar.

Oxygen Exposure

DAC systems operate in ~21% O₂. Oxygen at elevated regeneration temperatures causes oxidative amine degradation. Stability testing must be performed under simulated air, not just inert gas.

What to Report

  • Capacity and kinetics at multiple humidity levels (at minimum 0% and ~40–60% RH)
  • Capacity at multiple capture temperatures
  • Capacity across CO₂ partial pressure 0.35–0.45 mbar
  • Degradation under simulated air during both adsorption and regeneration
  • At least 10 cycles with first and last cycle capacities reported

Varying working capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy, and sorbent lifetime by ±30% can shift the modeled cost of CO₂ removal by hundreds of dollars per ton. Deployment-relevant testing is essential, not optional.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials alongside new sorbents is critical for measurement reliability and long-term comparability. Over 60% of sorbents in our database were reported in only a single publication, making it difficult to verify or reproduce results.

Selection Approach

Benchmark candidates were identified as commercially available materials that had been tested under the standard conditions for their sorbent class and had the highest deployment-relevance characterization among qualifying candidates.

Lewatit VP OC 1065
Amine
Polyamine-functionalized polymer bead from Lanxess. Most widely studied amine sorbent in the database and the reference for our technoeconomic sensitivity analysis.
ZIF-8
MOF
Zeolitic imidazolate framework (Zn-based). Commercially available, thermally stable, and among the most studied MOFs for gas adsorption.
γ-Al₂O₃
Metal oxide
Widely available metal oxide support with well-documented CO₂ adsorption properties under DAC conditions.
Zeolite 13X
Other
Commercially available sodium aluminosilicate zeolite, well-characterized for CO₂ adsorption and widely used in gas separation.

About

Methods, team, and citation information.

Overview

This database was compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid DAC sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included. This is a representative sample, not a complete catalog, and will be continuously updated.
