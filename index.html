Recorded Parameters

Each entry in the database corresponds to one experimental measurement of a sorbent under a specific set of conditions. A total of 18 parameters were recorded for each entry. Text and numerical values were extracted directly from each paper; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported are marked nr; values not applicable to a measurement type are marked na.

Sorbent Identity

Year

Year of publication of the paper in which the sorbent data was reported.

Sorbent Class

Classification based on the active CO₂-capture functionality. Amine: any material with an amine functional group that interacts with CO₂, regardless of the support type. MOF: crystalline metal–organic frameworks without amine CO₂-binding groups. Metal oxide: inorganic metal–oxygen compounds. Other: all remaining types including COFs, zeolites, ionic liquids, and moisture-swing sorbents.

Material Abbreviation

A short identifier for the sorbent using the abbreviation from the source paper. Additional descriptors (loading percentage, functional group type, etc.) are included where necessary to distinguish similar materials from the same study.

Performance Metrics

For detailed descriptions of CO₂ Adsorption Capacity, Degradation Rate, and Cycle Time — including their definitions, equations, and limitations — see the Trends page, where each metric is described under its respective tab.

Experimental Conditions

Capture Temperature

Temperature (°C) during the CO₂ adsorption phase. Sorbents reported only as "room temperature" or "ambient" were recorded as 25 °C.

Release Temperature

Temperature (°C) during regeneration. For vacuum-swing materials, this is the temperature at which vacuum was applied. For materials with activation/degassing rather than desorption, the activation temperature was recorded.

CO₂ Concentration

CO₂ concentration (ppm) in the feed gas during adsorption. Sorbents reported as "ambient air" were recorded as 425 ppm. Only sorbents tested below 5,000 ppm were included.

Capture Gas

Whether oxygen was present in the capture gas during adsorption. N (Inert + CO₂): inert carrier (N₂ or He) with CO₂, no oxygen. Y (Air): feed gas contains O₂, representing simulated or real ambient air. Oxygen can drive oxidative degradation of amine sorbents, so sorbents tested only under inert gas may report overestimated stabilities.

Regeneration Gas

Gas used during desorption or regeneration. Common values include N₂, He, Ar, vacuum, and mixtures containing CO₂ or H₂O. Recorded as reported in the paper.

Relative Humidity

Relative humidity (%) of the capture feed gas during adsorption. Also converted to mole percent water vapor and dew point (°C) using the adsorption temperature. A value of 0% indicates fully dry conditions.

Number of Cycles

Total adsorption–desorption cycles performed. Zero indicates a single adsorption measurement with no cycling. Approximately 20% of sorbents in the database report any cycling data.

Degradation Cause

Mechanism of degradation as reported or suggested in the paper. Categories include Thermal, Oxidation, Hydrolysis, CO₂-catalyzed, Pore blockage, Structural, Negligible degradation, No mechanism suggested, and combinations. If multiple causes were reported, all are listed.

CO₂ Measurement Method

Gravimetric (TGA): mass change tracked in a thermogravimetric analyzer. Breakthrough: CO₂ outlet concentration monitored in a packed-bed column; governed by both intrinsic kinetics and column hydrodynamics. Volumetric (Isotherm): equilibrium uptake at defined partial pressures. Other: all remaining methods.

Gas Flow Rate

Volumetric flow rate (mL/min) during adsorption. Not applicable for isotherm measurements. Large variation across studies — amines typically 100–500 mL/min, MOFs often below 50 mL/min — complicates direct kinetic comparisons.

Time to 95% Outlet Concentration (t₉₅)

Time (min) for breakthrough column CO₂ outlet to reach 95% of inlet concentration. The primary kinetic metric from breakthrough experiments, used to calculate standardized cycle time.

Column Diameter

Inner diameter (cm) of the breakthrough column. Required, with mass loading and flow rate, to normalize kinetic data across different reactor geometries.

Mass of Sorbent

Mass (g) of sorbent loaded into the column or TGA pan. Ranges from <0.1 g to >10 g across studies, significantly affecting reported adsorption times.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability and deployment-relevance of DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

A key challenge in assessing progress in DAC sorbent development is the wide variation in testing conditions used across studies. Without a consistent testing framework, differences in reported performance may reflect experimental variation rather than true material improvements.

When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, every class appears to show a positive trend over time. But once only sorbents tested under the same standard conditions are isolated, only amine sorbents show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries under standard conditions to analyze.

Standard conditions were determined by identifying the most frequently reported (mode) value for each parameter within each sorbent class. The percentage in parentheses indicates the fraction of that sorbent class tested under that condition.

How Many Sorbents Meet Standard Conditions?

Applying the standard conditions as sequential filters reveals how quickly the dataset narrows. For all 914 sorbents combined, only 335 (37%) satisfy all standard conditions simultaneously. For amine sorbents, 312 of 710 (44%) qualify. For MOF sorbents, only 4 of 42 (10%) do. For metal oxide sorbents, 15 of 56 (27%) qualify, but zero have sufficient breakthrough data to calculate cycle time.

Reporting Kinetics and Degradation

CO₂ capacity is available for ~96% of sorbents, but cycle time can only be calculated for ~12% and degradation rate for only ~4%. These metrics do not require additional experiments — they can be derived from existing data if key parameters are also reported. We strongly encourage researchers to report flow rate, mass loading, and t₉₅ alongside breakthrough capacity measurements, and to extend cycling experiments to at least 10 cycles.

Recommendation: Adopt standard conditions analogous to NREL's photovoltaic testing program. Always report all experimental conditions alongside performance data — even small differences can substantially alter measured values.

Deployment-Relevant Reporting

While standardized testing enables comparability, real DAC systems will operate under a wide range of environmental conditions. A sensitivity analysis on Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying humidity, ambient temperature, and altitude across U.S. deployment sites can shift the modeled net CO₂ removal cost by more than $400 per ton.

Currently, only about 8% of sorbents in the database have been characterized under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative gas environments, humid gas environments, and breakthrough measurements.

Key Environmental Factors

Relative Humidity

At moderate humidity (20–60% RH), water can increase CO₂ capacity of amine sorbents by shifting the mechanism toward ammonium bicarbonate formation. At high humidity, excess water loading raises regeneration energy and can block CO₂ binding sites.

Ambient Temperature

Lower temperatures increase CO₂ adsorption affinity but raise desorption energy requirements. Higher temperatures reduce capacity due to competitive water adsorption. DAC deployment sites range from hot-dry desert to cold subarctic conditions.

Altitude and CO₂ Partial Pressure

At 2,000 m elevation, the CO₂ partial pressure is ~0.34 mbar vs. ~0.42 mbar at sea level, reducing adsorption driving force. We recommend reporting CO₂ isotherms across 0.35–0.45 mbar to capture conditions relevant to both high-altitude and sea-level sites.

Oxygen Exposure

DAC systems operate in ambient air (~21% O₂). Oxygen at elevated regeneration temperatures can cause oxidative degradation of amine groups. Degradation testing should always be performed under simulated air during both adsorption and regeneration.

What to Report

  • CO₂ capacity and kinetics at multiple humidity levels (at minimum: 0% and ~40–60% RH)
  • CO₂ capacity at multiple capture temperatures
  • CO₂ capacity across a CO₂ partial pressure range of 0.35–0.45 mbar
  • Degradation testing under simulated air during both adsorption and regeneration
  • At least 10 adsorption–desorption cycles with first and last cycle capacities reported

Varying working CO₂ capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy, and sorbent lifetime by just ±30% can shift the modeled levelized cost of CO₂ removal by several hundred dollars per ton. Deployment-relevant testing is therefore essential for sorbents intended for real-world application.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials alongside new sorbents is critical for ensuring measurement reliability and long-term comparability. Over 60% of sorbents in our database were reported in only a single publication, making it difficult to verify or reproduce results.

Selection Approach

Benchmark candidates were selected by identifying commercially available materials that had been tested under the standard conditions for their sorbent class and had the highest deployment-relevance characterization among qualifying candidates.

Lewatit VP OC 1065
Amine
A polyamine-functionalized polymer bead from Lanxess. Most widely studied amine sorbent in the database and the reference for the technoeconomic sensitivity analysis in this work.
ZIF-8

γ-Al₂O₃

Widely available metal oxide support with well-documented CO₂ adsorption properties under DAC conditions.

About This Database

Methods, team, and citation information.

Overview

This database was compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid DAC sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included. This is a representative sample, not a complete catalog, and will be continuously updated.
