Recorded Parameters

Descriptions of all 18 parameters recorded for each sorbent entry in the database.

Data was collected manually from each publication. Text and numerical values were extracted directly from the paper; graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer. Values not reported in the paper are marked nr (not reported); values not applicable to a given measurement type are marked na (not applicable).

Performance metrics
CO₂ Adsorption Capacity
Adsorption capacity (mmol/g)
CO₂ uptake of the sorbent, converted to mmol CO₂ per gram of sorbent. Originally recorded in the units reported by each paper (mmol/g, wt%, g/g, etc.) and then converted. For isotherms, the capacity near 400 ppm CO₂ was used. Capacities that could not be converted (e.g. mmol/g-amine without amine loading) are marked nr.
Calculated
Cycle Time
Cycle time (Min)
A normalized metric to compare adsorption kinetics across different breakthrough reactor setups. Not directly recorded from papers — calculated from other reported parameters using the following approach:
CO₂ uptake efficiency = (CO₂ capacity × mass loading) / (t₉₅ × flow rate × CO₂ conc / 22.4)

Assuming uptake efficiency is independent of flow rate, mass loading, and CO₂ conc, t₉₅ is modeled for a standard reactor:
STD: mass = 100 mg, flow = 400 mL/min, CO₂ = 400 ppm

Cycle time = t₉₅_std × 3
Only calculable for breakthrough experiments that report t₉₅, mass loading, flow rate, CO₂ concentration, and CO₂ capacity.
Degradation Rate (%/cycle)
Degradation rate (%/cycle)
Average percent reduction in CO₂ capacity per adsorption–desorption cycle. Calculated as:
Degradation rate (%) = [(capacity₁ − capacity_last) / capacity₁] / N_cycles × 100
Only reported for sorbents tested for ≥2 cycles where both first and last cycle capacities are given. Does not account for nonlinear degradation over cycles.
Sorbent identity
Year
Year
Year of publication of the paper in which the sorbent was reported.
Sorbent Class
Sorbent Class
Classification of the sorbent based on the active CO₂-capture functionality. Amine: any material with an amine group interacting with CO₂, regardless of support. MOF: crystalline metal–organic frameworks without amine CO₂-binding groups. Metal oxide: inorganic metal–oxygen compounds. Other: all remaining types (COFs, zeolites, ionic liquids, etc.).
Experimental conditions
Capture Temperature
Capture temperature (C)
Temperature (°C) used during the CO₂ adsorption phase. For sorbents reported only as "room temperature" or "ambient," 25 °C was used.
Release Temperature
Release temperature (C)
Temperature (°C) used during the regeneration/desorption phase. The activation or degassing temperature was used for materials that did not undergo cyclic desorption. For vacuum-swing materials, this is the temperature at which vacuum was applied.
CO₂ Concentration
CO2 Concentration (ppm)
CO₂ concentration (ppm) in the feed gas during the adsorption phase. For sorbents reported only as "ambient air," 425 ppm was used. Only sorbents tested below 5,000 ppm were included in the database.
Capture Gas
Simulated air? (Y/N)…
Whether O₂ was present in the capture gas during adsorption. N = Inert + CO₂ (the gas is an inert carrier such as N₂ or He with CO₂ added, no oxygen). Y = Air (the gas contains O₂, i.e., simulated or real ambient air). This distinction matters because O₂ can accelerate oxidative degradation of amine sorbents.
Regeneration Gas
Purging gas
The gas used during the desorption/regeneration phase. Common values include N₂, He, Ar (inert), vacuum, or gas mixtures containing CO₂ or H₂O. Recorded as reported in the paper.
Relative Humidity
Moisture content (RH%)
Relative humidity (%) of the capture gas during adsorption. Also recorded as mole percent water vapor and dew point (°C), calculated from RH% and capture temperature. A value of 0 indicates dry conditions.
Number of Cycles
Capture-release cycles tested (#)
Total number of adsorption–desorption cycles tested. A value of 0 indicates no cycling was performed (capacity from a single adsorption measurement). Only ~20% of sorbents in the database report any cycling data.
Degradation Cause
Degradation cause (Category)
Categorized mechanism of degradation as reported or suggested in the paper. Categories include: Thermal, Oxidation, Hydrolysis, CO₂-catalyzed, Pore blockage, Structural, Negligible degradation, No mechanism suggested, and combinations thereof. If multiple causes were reported, the sorbent is classified with all relevant categories.
CO₂ Measurement Method
Method of CO2 capacity measurement
Experimental technique used to measure CO₂ uptake. Gravimetric (TGA): mass change tracked over time in a thermogravimetric analyzer. Breakthrough: CO₂ outlet concentration monitored as gas flows through a packed bed column. Volumetric (Isotherm): equilibrium CO₂ uptake measured volumetrically at defined pressures. Other: all other methods.
Gas Flow Rate
Gas flow rate during adsorption (mL/min)
Volumetric flow rate (mL/min) of capture gas during adsorption. Only applicable and reported for breakthrough and TGA experiments; not applicable for isotherm measurements.
Time to 95% Outlet Concentration (t₉₅)
Time to reach 95% outlet concentration (min)
Time (min) for the CO₂ outlet concentration of a breakthrough column to reach 95% of the inlet concentration. This is the saturation time used to calculate cycle time. Only applicable to breakthrough experiments.
Column Diameter
Column Diameter (cm)
Inner diameter (cm) of the breakthrough column. Only applicable to breakthrough experiments. Needed alongside mass loading and flow rate to normalize kinetic data across different reactor geometries.
Mass of Sorbent
Mass of sorbent loaded (g)
Mass (g) of sorbent loaded in the column or TGA sample pan. Used in normalization of cycle time calculations for breakthrough experiments. Wide variation in this parameter (from <0.1 g to >10 g) significantly impacts reported adsorption times.

Reporting Guidelines

Recommendations from our database analysis to improve comparability across DAC sorbent studies.

Standardized Testing Conditions

Analysis of over 900 sorbents in the database revealed significant variation in testing protocols, complicating direct comparison of CO₂ capacity, kinetics, and degradation across studies. To determine the recommended standard conditions, we identified the mode (most frequently reported) value for each experimental parameter and sorbent class.

It is important to use standard testing conditions to avoid drawing inaccurate conclusions. When CO₂ capacity is plotted for all sorbents under all conditions, all classes show a positive trend over time. But once only sorbents tested under the same standard conditions are isolated, only amines show a statistically meaningful positive trend — MOF and metal oxide sorbents have too few entries under standard conditions to analyze. This underscores why standard conditions are needed to fill the database.

Why standardization matters

This approach follows the same philosophy used in other clean-energy fields. For example, NREL's photovoltaic testing program adopted well-defined reference conditions (AM 1.5 G spectrum, 25 °C, 1000 W/m²) that enabled accurate, reproducible comparison of solar cell efficiencies across institutions worldwide. Applying a similar approach to DAC sorbents would create a common benchmark, reduce measurement uncertainty, and build confidence in reported performance metrics.

Key recommendation: Always report all experimental conditions alongside performance data. Even small differences in capture temperature, humidity, or CO₂ concentration can substantially alter measured capacities, making it impossible to compare sorbents across studies without this information.

What is currently under-reported?

CO₂ capacity is reported for ~96% of sorbents in the database, but cycle time can only be calculated for ~12% and degradation rate is available for only ~4%. These metrics can be measured with the same equipment used for capacity — simply extending the number of cycles and reporting the necessary breakthrough parameters (mass, flow rate, t₉₅) would make cycle time and degradation rate calculable for far more entries.

Deployment-Relevance (DR) Score

While standardized testing enables comparability across studies, real DAC systems will operate under a wide range of environmental conditions — different climates, altitudes, and oxygen levels. The Deployment-Relevance (DR) score quantifies how well a sorbent is characterized for accurate technoeconomic analysis at a given DAC deployment site.

A sensitivity analysis on Lewatit VP OC 1065 showed that varying key sorbent properties (CO₂ capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy, sorbent lifetime) by ±30% can shift the modeled levelized cost of CO₂ removal by several hundred dollars per ton. This underscores how important it is to accurately characterize these parameters under realistic operating conditions.

How the DR score is calculated

The DR score is the sum of points awarded for reporting the following data. A higher score means the sorbent is better characterized for deployment modeling.

+5Reports CO₂ capacity with all relevant experimental parameters (capture temp, regen temp, CO₂ concentration, relative humidity, regeneration gas)
+3for also reporting a CO₂ isotherm
+1for each additional capture temperature tested (max +3)
+1for each additional relative humidity tested (max +3)
+5Reports cycle time with relevant parameters (flow rate, mass loading, t₉₅)
+3if ≥2 humidity levels tested
+3if ≥2 capture temperatures tested
+3if ≥2 CO₂ concentrations tested
+5Reports degradation rate (≥2 cycles, CO₂ capacity of first and last cycle reported)
+3if ≥10 cycles tested
+3if tested with O₂ present
+3if tested at ≥2 humidity levels
+3if tested with CO₂ present during regeneration

Why deployment-relevant testing matters

DAC hubs are located across diverse U.S. climate zones, representing a broad spectrum of operating conditions. Environmental variations — humidity, ambient temperature, and altitude — can shift the modeled CO₂ capture cost by more than $400 per ton for the same sorbent. This is because these factors directly affect working CO₂ capacity, cycle time, regeneration energy demand, and sorbent lifetime.

  • Relative humidity: at moderate RH (20–60%), amine sorbents tend to show increased productivity as bicarbonate formation improves amine efficiency; at very high RH, excess water loading increases regeneration energy requirements.
  • Ambient temperature: lower temperatures increase CO₂ adsorption affinity and capacity but raise heating requirements for desorption; higher temperatures reduce capacity as water adsorption competes.
  • Altitude: at 2,000 m elevation, the partial pressure of CO₂ is roughly 0.34 mbar vs. 0.42 mbar at sea level, reducing adsorption driving force and uptake rates. Testing CO₂ isotherms from 0.35–0.45 mbar captures conditions from high-altitude to sea-level sites.
  • Oxygen exposure: DAC operates under ~21% O₂. Sorbents tested only under inert gas during regeneration may report overestimated stabilities. Reporting degradation under simulated air gives a more realistic stability assessment.

Note on current database coverage: only ~8% of sorbents in the database have been tested under all four deployment-relevant conditions: atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, oxidative gas environments, humid gas environments, and breakthrough measurements. This highlights a major gap in current DAC sorbent characterization.

Recommended Benchmark Materials

Including well-characterized reference materials in each study is critical for ensuring measurement reliability and enabling long-term comparability across laboratories. Over 60% of sorbents in the database were reported only once, making it difficult to verify the accuracy of measured CO₂ capacities or reproduce published results.

Incorporating standardized reference sorbents alongside new materials can validate equipment performance, confirm data consistency, and identify systematic errors. Repeated testing of the same benchmark across laboratories establishes a network of reference data that anchors new measurements to a common standard.

Selection criteria

Benchmark materials were selected using a data-driven approach applied to the database:

  • Must be commercially available
  • Must be priced below $1,000/g (to be accessible to most research groups)
  • Must have been tested under the standard conditions for its sorbent class
  • Out of sorbents meeting the above criteria, the one with the highest Deployment-Relevance (DR) score was selected for each class

Recommended benchmarks

Lewatit VP OC 1065
Amine
A commercially available polyamine-functionalized polymer bead from Lanxess. The most widely studied sorbent in the database and the basis for the technoeconomic sensitivity analysis in this work. Strong candidate as the primary amine benchmark.
ZIF-8
MOF
A zeolitic imidazolate framework (Zn-based) that is commercially available, thermally stable, and widely studied. Recommended as the MOF benchmark due to availability and breadth of existing characterization data.
γ-Al₂O₃
Metal oxide
Gamma-alumina is a widely available metal oxide support with well-documented surface chemistry. Its CO₂ adsorption properties under DAC conditions are established and can serve as a metal oxide reference point.
Zeolite 13X
Other
A commercially available sodium aluminosilicate zeolite. Widely used in gas separation and well-characterized for CO₂ adsorption. Recommended as the benchmark for the "other" sorbent class.

Why include reference materials?

Establishing reference sorbents follows the same philosophy used successfully in photovoltaics, where NREL's adoption of reference conditions (AM 1.5 G spectrum, 25 °C, 1000 W/m²) and certified reference cells enabled accurate, reproducible comparison of solar cell efficiencies across institutions worldwide. Applying a similar approach to DAC sorbents would provide the same benefits: a benchmark for comparison, faster identification of genuine performance improvements, and reduced measurement uncertainty.

About This Database

Methods, team, citation, and contribution information.

Overview

This database was compiled as part of a study on benchmarking strategies for solid direct air capture (DAC) sorbents. A SciFinder search for "Direct Air Capture" yielded 1,242 publications as of October 2024. After screening for relevance and experimental CO₂ capacity data below 5,000 ppm, 163 papers totalling over 900 unique sorbent entries were included.

For each entry, 18 sorbent properties and experimental parameters were manually recorded, including sorbent name and type, CO₂ capacity, capture and release temperatures, CO₂ concentration, gas flow rate, humidity, adsorption kinetics, regeneration method, cycling stability, and degradation cause. Graphical data was digitized using WebPlotDigitizer.
